Regression Models in Dental Research

Regression analysis examines relationships between variables and predicts outcomes. In dental research, regression helps estimate how risk factors such as age, smoking, oral hygiene, or systemic conditions influence oral health outcomes.

Why use regression?

Regression models answer causal or predictive questions, for example: Does smoking increase the risk of periodontal disease? Does age predict clinical attachment loss? What factors influence implant failure?

Linear Regression

Use linear regression when the outcome is continuous (e.g., pocket depth, plaque index, salivary biomarker levels).

Model form: Y = β₀ + β₁X + ε

Interpretation: β₁ is the change in Y for a one-unit change in X. Example: If β₁ = 0.04 for age predicting CAL, each additional year of age increases attachment loss by 0.04 mm on average.

Multiple Linear Regression

When multiple predictors are relevant, use multiple linear regression to estimate independent effects while adjusting for confounders.

Example: PocketDepth = β₀ + β₁(Age) + β₂(Smoking) + β₃(PlaqueIndex) + ε

This model estimates the effect of each predictor holding the others constant.

Logistic Regression

Use logistic regression when the outcome is binary (e.g., implant success vs failure, disease present vs absent). The model estimates log-odds and yields odds ratios (OR).

Model form: ln(P / (1−P)) = β₀ + β₁X

Interpretation: An OR > 1 indicates higher odds of the outcome per unit increase in the predictor. Example: OR for smoking = 2.5 → smokers have 2.5× the odds of implant failure vs non-smokers.

Model Assumptions

  • Independence: Observations should be independent (use clustered or multilevel models when they are not).
  • Linearity (for linear regression): Relationship between predictors and outcome should be linear; otherwise consider transformations or nonlinear models.
  • No multicollinearity: Predictors should not be highly correlated. Use VIF to detect multicollinearity (rule of thumb: VIF < 10).
  • Adequate sample size: Small samples produce unstable estimates; for logistic regression follow EPV ≥ 10 (events per predictor).

Practical guidance

Check assumptions, report regression coefficients (β) with confidence intervals, and for logistic models report odds ratios (OR) with 95% CIs. Adjust for confounders and consider interaction terms when appropriate.

Applications in dental research

Common applications include identifying risk factors for periodontal disease, predicting implant outcomes, studying determinants of caries, and evaluating the effect of oral hygiene behaviors while controlling for sociodemographic factors.
