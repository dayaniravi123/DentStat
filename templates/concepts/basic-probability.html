Foundations

Events, Outcomes, and Complements

Sample space and events

Probability begins with a sample space: the full set of outcomes a random experiment can produce. An event is any subset of that sample space. If you toss three fair coins, the sample space contains eight outcomes such as HHH, HHT, and TTT. If you care about “at least two heads”, you only collect the outcomes that match that question.

In dental research, the same thinking appears when we define events like “implant survives one year”, “DMFT score is at least 3”, or “a patient reports high satisfaction”. The event is the question; the probability tells us how often it should happen in the long run.

Complements simplify many questions

A complement is the event that something does not happen. This is often the quickest route to an answer. If an implant succeeds with probability 0.95, then failure has probability 0.05. Many “at least one” questions are easier to solve by first finding the probability of “none”.

0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1   ·   P(Aᶜ) = 1 − P(A)
  • P(A)=0 means impossible; P(A)=1 means certain.
  • The sample space changes with the experiment, but every event always lives inside that space.
  • Complements are especially useful for failure probabilities, dropout probabilities, and “at least one” situations.
  • The Law of Large Numbers explains why observed proportions get steadier as data accumulates.
Useful Example from the Chapter

In the chapter’s three-coin experiment, the event “at least two heads” is not a single outcome; it is a collection of outcomes. That is the core shift in probability: we rarely care about one raw result in isolation, we care about sets of results that answer a question.

2
Conditioning

Conditional Probability

What changes when new information arrives?

Conditional probability asks: how does the probability of one event change once we already know another event has happened? The important idea is that the sample space shrinks. Once new information arrives, you stop counting impossible cases and focus only on the cases still compatible with that information.

This is why conditional probability is so useful in screening, diagnosis, and research interpretation. “What is the chance of disease?” is different from “what is the chance of disease given a positive test?” The second question is always about a filtered set of patients.

P(E|F) = P(E ∩ F) / P(F)
  • Conditioning does not create new outcomes; it removes outcomes that no longer fit the information you have.
  • The denominator is the event you are conditioning on, so it must have nonzero probability.
  • This idea is the doorway to Bayes’ theorem, where you reverse the direction of a condition.
  • Students often improve fastest when they redraw the problem with the smaller sample space first.
Useful Example from the Chapter

Ten cards numbered 1 to 10 are mixed, and you are told the drawn card is greater than 3. The probability that it is even is not based on all ten cards anymore. You now work only with {4,5,6,7,8,9,10}, so the answer becomes 4/7.

Why it matters in research

Conditional thinking sits behind diagnostic reasoning, follow-up analyses, subgroup reporting, and Bayesian updates.

Good student habit

Write “given that …” as a new mini-sample-space before touching the formula. It prevents most denominator mistakes.

3
Sequences

Multiplication Rule and Tree Thinking

Chaining probabilities across steps

When you need the probability that two events happen together, conditional probability naturally leads to the multiplication rule. This is the rule you use for multi-step procedures, sequential card draws, repeated screening stages, or any process where one step changes the next.

Tree diagrams are especially helpful here because they force you to read paths one branch at a time. The chapter uses a nice example where a coin is tossed and, depending on the result, you either toss again or throw a die. Tree thinking makes the conditional structure visible before you calculate anything.

P(E ∩ F) = P(E) · P(F|E)
  • Without replacement, the second step often depends on the first, so conditional probability is essential.
  • With replacement, the next step may keep the same probabilities, which often simplifies the calculation.
  • For three or more stages, keep multiplying by the next conditional probability on the path you care about.
  • Tree diagrams are often the clearest way to avoid mixing up branches and totals.
Useful Example from the Chapter

An urn has 10 black and 5 white balls. If two balls are drawn without replacement, the probability that both are black is (10/15) × (9/14) = 3/7. The second fraction changes because the first draw changed the urn.

4
Independence

Independent Events

When one event does not change the other

Two events are independent when learning that one happened does not alter the probability of the other. This is a probability idea, not just a logic idea. In symbols, independence means the overlap behaves exactly like the product of the separate probabilities.

P(E ∩ F) = P(E) · P(F)
  • Independence is different from being mutually exclusive.
  • If two nonzero-probability events are mutually exclusive, they cannot be independent.
  • Repeated fair die throws and fair coin tosses are classic independent trials.
  • Many students test independence fastest by checking whether P(E ∩ F) equals P(E)P(F).
Useful Example from the Chapter

On one die throw, let E be “multiple of 3” and F be “even”. Then E = {3,6}, F = {2,4,6}, and E ∩ F = {6}. Since P(E)=1/3, P(F)=1/2, and P(E ∩ F)=1/6, the product rule matches exactly, so the events are independent.

Research translation

Independence assumptions appear constantly in repeated trials, random sampling, and many standard probability models.

What to watch for

If one step changes the pool, the dosage, or the group you are sampling from, independence usually disappears.

5
Distributions

Random Variables, Expectation, and Variance

From outcomes to numbers

A random variable assigns a numerical value to each outcome in the sample space. Once outcomes are converted into numbers, you can build a probability distribution, which tells you which values are possible and how much probability each one gets.

Expected value

The expected value is the probability-weighted average of those values. It tells you the long-run centre of the distribution, even if no single patient ever lands exactly on that average.

E(X) = Σ xP(x)

Variance

The mean tells you where the distribution is centred; variance tells you how widely it spreads around that centre. This is why two treatments can have similar averages but very different predictability.

Var(X) = E[(X - μ)2] = E(X2) - [E(X)]2
  • Expected value is a weighted average, not just an arithmetic average.
  • Variance is always non-negative; standard deviation is its square root.
  • DMFT distributions, prevalence scores, and rating scales all fit naturally into this framework.
  • Mean answers “where is the centre?”; variance answers “how stable is the outcome?”.
Dental Translation

If DMFT scores of 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 occur with probabilities 0.30, 0.25, 0.20, 0.12, 0.08, and 0.05, the expected DMFT is the weighted sum of those scores. A second community could have a similar expected value but a much larger variance, meaning disease burden is less predictable from patient to patient.

Repeated Trials

Bernoulli Trials and Binomial Distribution

When the same success/failure trial repeats

Bernoulli trials are repeated experiments with exactly two outcomes, usually labelled success and failure, where each trial stays independent and the probability of success remains constant. This makes them perfect for thinking about repeated yes/no outcomes.

  • Finite number of trials.
  • Exactly two outcomes on each trial.
  • Trials are independent.
  • The probability of success stays the same each time.

Binomial thinking

If X counts the number of successes in n Bernoulli trials, then X follows a binomial distribution. The chapter uses this to count exact numbers of heads, defective items, or other repeated outcomes.

P(X = x) = nCx px qn-x   where   q = 1 - p
Student-Friendly Dental Version

If each implant has a 95% success probability and you observe 10 independent implants, then the chance of at least one failure is easier to find by complement: 1 - (0.95)10. This is exactly the same logic as the chapter’s “at least one defective item” binomial example.

Use this model when

You are counting how many successes happen across repeated identical yes/no trials.

Do not use it when

The success probability changes between trials or one outcome changes the pool for the next draw.
