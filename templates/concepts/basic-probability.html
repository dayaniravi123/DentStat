Basic Probability

Probability tells us how likely an event is to happen. In dental research, it helps us reason about implant success, DMFT outcomes, and patient satisfaction with clearer intuition before moving into more advanced statistical ideas.

1
Chance Events

Understanding Probability

Probability is a number between 0 and 1 indicating how likely an event will occur. In dentistry, implant procedures, root canals, and fillings each carry specific success rates backed by clinical research.

Will this implant integrate successfully? Will a root canal relieve the patient's pain? These are questions of probability. By simulating many outcomes, we observe the Law of Large Numbers in action — observed frequencies converge to the true probability as the number of trials grows.

0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1   ·   P(Aᶜ) = 1 − P(A)
  • P(A) = 0 means the event is impossible; P(A) = 1 means it is certain.
  • The complement rule: if an implant has a 95% success rate, the failure rate is 1 − 0.95 = 0.05.
  • The Law of Large Numbers guarantees that observed proportions converge to the true probability as sample size increases.
  • Clinical success rates (e.g., implant: 95%, filling: 90%) are derived from large-scale studies using this principle.
Dental Example

A dental implant has a success rate of P = 0.95. If a clinic places 200 implants, the expected number of successful outcomes is 200 × 0.95 = 190. The probability of failure per implant is 1 − 0.95 = 0.05, so roughly 10 failures are expected.

2
Expectation

Expected Value

The expected value (mean) is the long-run average of outcomes, weighted by their probabilities. The DMFT index tracks Decayed, Missing, and Filled Teeth — a key measure in dental epidemiology.

Different populations show different DMFT distributions. A healthy group clusters near 0, while a high-risk group skews toward higher scores. The expected value tells us the “center of gravity” of the distribution.

E[X] = Σ x · P(x)
  • Expected value is a weighted average — outcomes that are more likely contribute more.
  • It does not have to be a value that is actually observable. For example, E[DMFT] = 3.2 even though no patient has exactly 3.2 teeth affected.
  • The sample mean converges to E[X] as more observations are collected.
  • Comparing expected values across populations helps identify high-risk groups for targeted intervention.
Dental Example

In a community screening, suppose DMFT scores of 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 occur with probabilities 0.30, 0.25, 0.20, 0.12, 0.08, 0.05. The expected DMFT is 0(0.30) + 1(0.25) + 2(0.20) + 3(0.12) + 4(0.08) + 5(0.05) = 1.58. This tells us the average disease burden in the population.

3
Variance

Variance & Spread

Variance measures how spread out outcomes are from the expected value. In dentistry, patient satisfaction varies widely — some patients report excellent outcomes, others experience complications.

A low variance means consistent satisfaction; high variance signals unpredictable results. Understanding this spread is crucial for informed consent and treatment planning.

Var(X) = E[(X − E[X])²]
  • Variance is always non-negative. A variance of 0 means every outcome is identical.
  • Standard deviation (σ) is the square root of variance — it is in the same units as the data, making it easier to interpret.
  • When comparing treatments, a procedure with the same average outcome but lower variance is generally more predictable and preferred.
  • High variance in satisfaction scores may indicate that patient experience depends on factors not captured in the average.
Dental Example

Clinic A reports patient satisfaction scores of {8, 8, 9, 8, 9} (low variance), while Clinic B reports {3, 10, 5, 9, 8} (high variance). Both have similar averages (~8.4 vs 7.0), but Clinic A delivers more consistent experiences. Variance captures this difference that the mean alone cannot.
