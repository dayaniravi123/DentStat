Section 1

Chance Events

Probability is a number indicating how likely an event will occur. This number is always between 0 and 1, where 0 indicates impossibility and 1 indicates certainty.

In dentistry, we deal with chance every day. Will this patient develop caries this year? Does a positive screening test truly indicate disease? These are questions of probability.

0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1   ·   P(Aᶜ) = 1 − P(A)

Section 2

Expectation

The expected value (or mean) of a random variable is the long-run average of its outcomes, weighted by their probabilities.

In dental research, the DMFT index (Decayed, Missing, Filled Teeth) is a random variable. If we know the distribution of DMFT scores in a population, we can compute the expected DMFT a randomly selected patient will have.

E[X] = Σ x · P(x)

Section 3

Variance

Variance measures how spread out a distribution is — how much values deviate from the expected value on average. A high variance means outcomes are unpredictable; low variance means they are consistent.

In clinical dentistry, treatment outcomes vary: some patients heal quickly, others have complications. Variance helps quantify this spread — essential for informed consent and treatment planning.

Var(X) = E[(X − E[X])²]
